ShopMakers —
Brand Card & Blog System

Overview

A small Gastown store. A big disconnect.

ShopMakers empowers local artisans by giving them shelf space and letting them keep 100% of sales. But customers walking in had no way to connect with the person behind the product — no story, no context, no bridge between maker and customer.

The ShopMakers website only served potential makers, completely ignoring existing customers. As project lead, I directed the team using a double diamond process, personally led the client interview, and handled the primary design work.

I went to the store. Then I interviewed the manager.

Understanding both sides

I mapped both the customer and the maker.

Most teams only think about the end user. I mapped both sides of the ecosystem — what the customer experiences and what the maker goes through — to find where the real gaps were.

Customer journey map — Tina Warden, SFU local student. Brand discovery, in-store friction, hidden service fees at checkout.

Maker journey map — Karen, local artisan. Loyalty program signup, in-store frustrations, lack of customer feedback channels.

Constraints

The store ruled out most obvious solutions.

Before designing anything, I had to understand what we couldn't do. These constraints shaped every decision I made and ruled out every flashy solution we brainstormed.

No space

Gastown location is compact — almost no room for physical displays or large interventions of any kind

No tech

Security concerns in the area ruled out tablet and screen solutions entirely — too expensive, too risky

Small team

Limited staff meant any solution had to be fully self-managing and low-maintenance for makers

Concept exploration

I explored multiple directions. Then merged the two best ones.

After a participatory workshop with the client, I narrowed to two strong directions — then realised combining them created something better than either alone. A connected system rather than a single isolated feature.

Concept 01 — Blog wireframes. Grid view for maker discovery, individual post view for brand stories and SEO.

The solution

A phygital connection system — not just a blog.

I merged both concepts into one connected system. The brand card bridges the in-store experience to the online one. Together they create a continuous journey from shelf to screen to repeat store visit.

01 — In store

The Brand Card

Physical card on each shelf with maker story, product info, social links, and a QR code. Low-cost, space-efficient, customisable per maker. Works within every constraint.

02 — Online

The Makers Blog

Blog on ShopMakers website accessible via nav and QR code. Makers share stories, customers ask questions, leave reviews, and find store locations.

Why a physical card instead of a digital-only solution?

Security and space constraints ruled out screens. The brand card works within every limitation — no power, no maintenance, almost zero cost. It meets customers exactly where they already are: standing at the shelf.

Why a blog and not a product listing page?

A product listing duplicates what makers already have on their own socials. A blog lets makers tell their story — what customers actually wanted. It also benefits ShopMakers' SEO, solving two problems with one feature.

Why connect the two with a QR code?

The QR code creates a seamless bridge between physical and digital without requiring any staff involvement. Customers who are curious can go deeper on their own time, even after leaving the store. It turns a one-time visit into an ongoing relationship.

Why use the existing ShopMakers brand?

The client was a small operation with limited resources. Using their existing brand meant zero additional cost and immediate consistency across all touchpoints. My job was to enhance what existed, not replace it.

What I learned

Good design doesn't start with ideas — it starts with constraints. Every flashy solution I brainstormed got ruled out by the reality of the store. What worked was designing within the limitations, not around them. The blog idea only made sense after I understood the website was failing customers entirely. Without that interview, I never would have found it.
